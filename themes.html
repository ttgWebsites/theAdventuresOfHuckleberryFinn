Themes

  • Developing intellectually and morally
  • Growing up Huck did not have the typical family situation as other children in his town, he was raised by a drunk father and his mother was long gone
  • Huck was not taught proper etiquette and was not very well educated
  • Following The Adventures of Tom Sawyer Huck is put in the care of Widow Douglas
  • She tries to raise him with what her ideas of good morals and ideals are however Huck distrusts her and society as well
  • Huck manages to grow up by his own experiences, he puts asides the teachings and urgings of others and learns by himself as well as through Jim
  • Huck sees that society is quite wrong with how they treat people, particularly slaves, and while separated from society's influences on the raft he discovers right and wrong, and builds a strong moral character by himself
  • The hypocritical nature of a "Civilized" society
  • Huck's main goal in this novel is to escape civilization
  • He hates being forced to bathe, go to school, and act orderly
  • However after reading the novel, it is hard to consider the town Huck lives in much of a civilized place
  • Huck's abusive and alcoholic father is allowed to regain custody of Huck, men are treated as property, and their are often lynching for ridiculous crimes
  • Twain often reiterates that it is hard to call a society civilizes when slavery occurs
  • Racism
  • The novel is set before the civil war, even though it was written two decades after when slavery had ended
  • Even after the end of slavery, black were still treated unfairly and were no where near equal
  • It seems Twain has a problem with slavery and racism, and he makes it clear in his writing by remarking about slavery in a negative way
  • Huck develops a friendship with Jim, a runaway slave, and Huck does not seem to view him any differently than he would a white man
  • Huck represents a minority during that time period
